### Gemini

That looks like a significant bruise, which often points to a strain or partial tear of the medial hamstrings (specifically the semitendinosus or semimembranosus).

As a runner over 60, your muscle fibers have different elasticity than they once did. Sprinting‚Äîespecially the "snap" of full extension and the heavy eccentric load of braking‚Äîdemands massive force from these tendons. Since your standard leg curls aren't hitting the spot, you likely need eccentric-focused movements that mimic the "braking" action and movements that engage the hamstrings while the hip is extended.

Here are four exercises to target that specific area:

1. Nordic Hamstring Curls
This is the "gold standard" for preventing sprint-related injuries. It specifically trains the muscle to handle the force during the "braking" phase of your stride.

The Move: Kneel on a soft surface with your ankles secured (by a partner or under a heavy weight bench). Slowly lower your torso toward the floor, resisting gravity as long as possible using only your hamstrings. Catch yourself with your hands.

Why it works: It builds extreme eccentric strength, which is exactly where you are currently failing during deceleration.

Link: How to do Nordic Hamstring Curls

2. Single-Leg Romanian Deadlifts (RDLs)
Standard leg curls work the hamstrings via knee flexion, but sprinting requires them to work via hip extension.

The Move: Stand on one leg with a slight bend in the knee. Hinge at the hips, sending your butt back and lowering a weight (or just your hands) toward the floor while keeping your back flat.

Why it works: By doing this single-legged, you force the medial hamstrings to stabilize the knee, preventing the "pull" you feel on the inside of the leg.

Link: Single-Leg RDL Technique

3. Stability Ball Hamstring Curls
This bridges the gap between a weight machine and a sprint.

The Move: Lie on your back with your heels on a stability ball. Lift your hips into a bridge. While keeping your hips high, curl the ball toward your glutes and then‚Äîcrucially‚Äîslowly extend your legs back out.

Why it works: It requires the hamstrings to work while the hips are in extension, which mimics the posture of a runner much better than a seated machine.

Link: Stability Ball Hamstring Curls

4. Glute-Ham Bridge (with Isometric Holds)
If the muscle is currently injured, isometric (holding) exercises are a great way to build tendon resilience without the "snap" of movement.

The Move: Perform a glute bridge but walk your feet out further away from your body so the knees are only slightly bent. Hold this position for 30‚Äì60 seconds.

Why it works: Hamstring pulls often happen when the leg is nearly straight (full extension). This strengthens the muscle in that specific vulnerable "long" position.

Link: Long-Lever Isometric Bridge

### Claude

The muscle you're pulling is likely your semimembranosus or semitendinosus - the medial (inner) hamstrings. These are particularly vulnerable during the terminal swing phase of sprinting and during rapid deceleration because they work eccentrically to control knee extension and help brake your forward motion.
Here are 4 targeted exercises to strengthen this area:
1. Nordic Hamstring Curls (Eccentric Focus)

Kneel on a pad, anchor your ankles (partner holds them or use a loaded barbell)
Keep hips extended, slowly lower your torso forward as far as controlled
Use your hands to catch yourself, push back up to assist
Start with 3 sets of 3-5 reps, progress to 8-10 reps
This is the gold standard for hamstring injury prevention

2. Single-Leg Romanian Deadlifts (RDLs)

Hold a dumbbell in one hand, stand on opposite leg
Hinge at hip, keeping slight knee bend, lower weight toward floor
Focus on controlled descent and feeling stretch in hamstring
3 sets of 8-12 reps per leg
Builds eccentric strength in lengthened position

3. Slider/Swiss Ball Leg Curls

Lie on back, heels on sliders or swiss ball
Bridge hips up, then curl heels toward glutes
Slowly extend legs back out (eccentric phase is key)
To emphasize medial hamstrings: turn toes slightly outward
3 sets of 10-15 reps
Trains hamstrings through full range of motion

4. Terminal Knee Extension Drops (Eccentric)

Stand on step/box (6-12 inches)
Slowly lower one leg down behind you in a controlled manner
Keep working leg slightly bent, resist the eccentric lowering
3 sets of 10-12 reps per leg
Mimics the braking action that causes your pulls

Key principles for you:

Prioritize the eccentric (lowering) phase - that's where sprint injuries occur
Start conservatively and progress gradually over 4-6 weeks
Do these 2-3x per week on non-sprint days
Consider reducing sprint volume temporarily while building this strength base

Given your age and injury pattern, also ensure you're warming up thoroughly before sprints and consider limiting true max-effort sprints to once weekly until this area is more resilient.
